Taking Care of the Future Today

Who are we trying to help?

When thinking about the future it is important to think about who is going to keep society running. That means thinking about all students under the age of 18 who are getting ready to decide what their lives will look like after they finish high school.

As time goes on it seems less students in the United States are aiming towards receiving a higher education after high school. Some may take this as proof that students in younger generations are becoming lazier or less motivated for one reason or another. What is highly overlooked is how many factors contribute to a student’s decision to attend college or university, and just how little say they have in the final decision. By taking a closer look at the American public education system it becomes clear that most of the struggles students face stem back to the inadequate funding of schools (K-12) and a lack of community support.

Even with the steady decrease in college enrollment, which will only lead to negative outcomes for the higher education institutions themselves, it seems no one is eager to make a change. Some higher education institutions even feel as if they will be exempt from what is being called “The Great Interruption” by authors Bill Conely and Robert Massa. But what matters most is not the schools, but the students. Who are the students that are deciding to not attend college? What caused them to make their decision? It comes to no surprise that most of the students unable to attend college come from low income areas, where funding for schools and community involvement are almost nonexistent. In her article, “How Does Place Affect Education?”, Ruth Lupton explores the effects of location and community on a students education and notes, “Evidence from the Gautreaux programme in Chicago suggested that moving to a better neighborhood reduced school drop-out rates, and improved college participation rates” (Lupton, 2006). Obviously schools have the ability to support students in their path towards higher education, but it seems that the families of students have to go out of their way to seek out quality education, when it should be provided no matter where they live, or what their financial status is.

By breaking down the exact influence money and community have over a students education, it is possible to pinpoint where change needs to take place, and how it can be done.
